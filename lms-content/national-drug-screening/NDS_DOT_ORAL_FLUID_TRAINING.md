## üéì Qualification Requirements

### To Become a Qualified DOT Oral Fluid Collector:

**Step 1: Complete Training**
- Receive training on all steps to complete a collection correctly
- Learn proper completion and transmission of CCF (Custody and Control Form)
- Understand problem collections, fatal flaws, and correctable flaws
- Learn responsibility for maintaining integrity of collection process
- Understand privacy requirements for employees being tested
- Learn security of specimen procedures
- Avoid conduct or statements that could be viewed as offensive

**Step 2: Demonstrate Proficiency**
- Complete **5 consecutive error-free mock collections**
- Mock collections must be supervised by qualified trainer
- Available via webcam or in-person
- Required before you can perform actual DOT collections

**‚ö†Ô∏è Note:** DOT does NOT certify specimen collectors. The requirement is to be **QUALIFIED** to perform collections.

## üìã What is DOT Oral Fluid Testing?

### Overview:

**DOT Oral Fluid Drug Testing:**
- Alternative to urine drug testing
- Tests for same DOT 5-Panel drugs:
  - Marijuana (THC)
  - Cocaine
  - Amphetamines
  - Opioids
  - Phencyclidine (PCP)

**Benefits of Oral Fluid Testing:**
- ‚úÖ **Every collection is directly observed** (no same-gender issues)
- ‚úÖ **No bathroom requirements** (can be done anywhere)
- ‚úÖ **Shorter detection window** (recent drug use)
- ‚úÖ **Less opportunity for cheating**
- ‚úÖ **More convenient** for remote areas
- ‚úÖ **Better for post-accident and reasonable suspicion testing**

**When Oral Fluid is REQUIRED:**
- When direct observation collection is required AND
- Donor identifies as non-binary or transgender
- Must use oral fluid (not urine)

## üéØ Standing Orders for Oral Fluid Testing

### Recommended Standing Orders:

**When to Use Oral Fluid:**
1. Any drug testing collection that requires direct observation
2. Post-accident testing (shorter detection window)
3. Reasonable suspicion testing (recent drug use)
4. Remote locations where urine collection is difficult
5. After-hours testing
6. Shy bladder situations (eliminate 3-hour wait)
7. Non-binary or transgender donors requiring direct observation (REQUIRED)

**When to Use Urine:**
1. Pre-employment testing (standard)
2. Random testing (standard)
3. Return-to-duty testing
4. Follow-up testing
5. When oral fluid is not available

**Employer Decision:**
- Only employer has authority to choose specimen type
- Applicant, employee, or collector CANNOT choose
- Provide clear standing orders to collection facilities

## üìä Comparison: Oral Fluid vs. Urine

| Feature | Oral Fluid | Urine |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **Collection** | Directly observed (always) | Observed only when required |
| **Privacy** | No bathroom needed | Bathroom required |
| **Gender Issues** | None (non-gender specific) | Same-gender observer required |
| **Detection Window** | 24-48 hours (recent use) | 2-7 days (longer window) |
| **Cheating** | Very difficult | More opportunities |
| **Convenience** | High (anywhere, anytime) | Lower (bathroom, privacy) |
| **Cost** | Similar to urine | Standard |
| **Lab Certification** | Waiting for 2 labs | Fully operational |
| **Availability** | Not yet operational | Available now |
